# C7.8 Source Attribution & Citation Integrity

[Back to C07 Index](C07-Model-Behavior)

## Purpose

Source attribution and citation integrity controls ensure that when a system uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to ground its responses in source documents, the citations are genuine and verifiable. LLMs are known to fabricate citations — generating plausible-looking but nonexistent references, or citing real sources that do not actually support the claimed content. In RAG architectures, citations should be derived from retrieval metadata (which documents were actually retrieved) rather than generated by the model (which can hallucinate sources). This section addresses both attribution (linking responses to retrieved documents) and verification (confirming cited claims are actually supported by the cited source).

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Role | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|:----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **7.8.1** | **Verify that** responses generated using retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) include attribution to the source documents that grounded the response, and that attributions are derived from retrieval metadata rather than generated by the model. | 1 | D/V | **Citation fabrication and ungrounded claims.** Without attribution tied to retrieval metadata, the model may generate plausible-sounding citations to documents that were never retrieved, do not exist, or do not support the stated claims. Users then act on information they believe is sourced but is actually hallucinated. | Submit queries to a RAG-enabled system. Verify responses include source attribution (document title, URL, chunk reference, or similar identifier). Critically, confirm that the attribution is derived from the retrieval system's metadata (document IDs, chunk IDs returned by the vector search) rather than generated as free text by the model. Test by comparing cited sources against the retrieval log to verify consistency. | Implementation approaches: (1) include retrieved document metadata in the response template at the application layer (not generated by the model); (2) use function calling where the retrieval results are returned as structured data with attribution pre-attached; (3) post-process model output to replace model-generated citations with metadata from the retrieval step. The key architectural principle is that citation generation must be separated from content generation. |
| **7.8.2** | **Verify that** each sourced claim in a RAG-grounded response can be traced to a specific retrieved chunk, and that the system detects and flags responses where claims are not supported by any retrieved content before the response is served. | 3 | V | **Hallucination masked by real citations.** A model may cite a real retrieved document but make claims not actually contained in that document. The citation creates a false sense of grounding — the user sees a source and trusts the claim, but the claim is fabricated despite the citation being real. This is harder to detect than outright citation fabrication. | For a set of RAG-grounded responses, extract individual claims and their cited source chunks. Perform entailment checking: does the cited chunk actually support the specific claim? Tools like natural language inference (NLI) models can automate this. Verify the system performs this check before serving the response and flags or blocks responses where claims are unsupported by their cited chunks. | Level 3 because claim-level entailment verification is computationally expensive and technically challenging. Requires: (1) decomposing responses into atomic claims, (2) mapping each claim to the cited chunk, (3) running NLI or semantic similarity scoring between claim and chunk. Research tools include RAGAS faithfulness metrics, FActScore-style atomic claim verification, and NLI models (e.g., cross-encoder/nli-deberta-v3-base). Production deployment at scale is still nascent. |

## Related Standards & References

- [RAGAS (Retrieval Augmented Generation Assessment)](https://docs.ragas.io/) — framework for evaluating RAG pipelines including faithfulness (is the response supported by retrieved context?) and context relevancy
- [FActScore (Min et al., 2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14251) — fine-grained factual precision scoring that decomposes text into atomic claims and verifies each
- [OWASP LLM Top 10 — Overreliance](https://genai.owasp.org/) — risks from trusting model outputs including fabricated citations
- [Attributed QA (Bohnet et al., 2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2212.08037) — research on attributable question answering and citation verification
- AISVS C7.2 (Hallucination Detection & Mitigation) — complementary controls; C7.8 focuses specifically on RAG attribution while C7.2 addresses hallucination more broadly

## Open Research Questions

- How should attribution be presented for responses that synthesize information across multiple retrieved chunks? Should every sentence be individually attributed?
- What entailment threshold is appropriate for flagging unsupported claims — strict logical entailment, or a softer "consistent with" standard?
- How can claim-level verification be made fast enough for real-time serving without unacceptable latency?
- How should systems handle partial support — where a retrieved chunk supports part of a claim but not all of it?
- Should users be able to click through citations to view the original source chunk, and what are the security implications of exposing retrieval internals?
