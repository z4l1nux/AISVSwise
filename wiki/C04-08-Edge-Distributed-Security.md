# C4.8 Edge & Distributed AI Security

[Back to C04 Index](C04-Infrastructure.md)

## Purpose

Secure distributed AI deployments including edge computing, federated learning, and multi-site architectures. Edge and mobile AI deployments operate in fundamentally hostile environments -- devices are physically accessible to attackers, network connectivity is intermittent, compute resources are constrained, and models deployed to devices can be extracted, reverse-engineered, or tampered with. This section addresses the unique security challenges of running AI inference on devices outside the controlled data center perimeter.

---

## Threat Landscape (2024--2026)

The edge AI attack surface has expanded dramatically as on-device inference moves from simple classifiers to full LLMs running locally on phones, vehicles, robots, and industrial controllers. Key developments include:

### Model Extraction Is Now Practical at Scale

Researchers have demonstrated practical model extraction from production mobile applications. In documented attacks against apps using TensorFlow Lite, attackers used dynamic instrumentation (Frida) to intercept decrypted TFLite models at runtime, bypassing static encryption protections entirely. Studies show most mobile apps today leave their on-device models completely unprotected. A connected-vehicle platform suffered an API abuse attack that exfiltrated core autonomous-driving model parameters, resulting in direct economic losses of $12.7 million due to intellectual-property compromise.

### Side-Channel Attacks on Edge AI Hardware

CVE-2025-23270 (CVSS 7.1) demonstrated that neural network weights can be extracted from NVIDIA Jetson chips by measuring electromagnetic radiation during inference. This class of side-channel attack -- using power consumption, EM emissions, or timing analysis -- is particularly dangerous for edge devices that lack the physical security controls of data centers. The industry-standard Test Vector Leakage Assessment (TVLA) method uses Welch's t-test to detect statistical leakage in power traces, but most edge deployments do not implement such testing.

### Agentic Edge AI Introduces New Attack Layers

The convergence of autonomous AI agents with edge computing (in robots, autonomous vehicles, drones, and industrial systems) has introduced multi-layer attack surfaces spanning perception (sensor spoofing, camera blinding, ultrasonic audio injection), cognition (adversarial inputs, model poisoning via OTA updates), and actuation (command injection, control-channel hijacking). Adversarial perturbations added to smart-home camera input frames can elevate a detector's false-negative rate from 12% to over 60%.

### Federated Learning Remains Vulnerable to Sophisticated Poisoning

Recent research (CVPR 2025, NDSS 2025) demonstrates that attacks like PoisonedFL can defeat eight state-of-the-art FL defenses simultaneously, without requiring knowledge of the defense mechanism or access to genuine training data. The attack maintains consistent poisoning direction across rounds, whereas prior attacks' effects self-cancel. New defenses such as FedMP (multi-pronged anomaly detection on update magnitude and direction) and neuron-centric approaches (NC-FLD) show improved resilience even when 70% of participants are malicious, but the arms race continues.

### On-Device LLMs Expand the Attack Surface

On-device LLMs (running on mobile SoCs with 8--16 GB RAM) expose significantly more intellectual property than traditional edge classifiers. TZ-LLM is the first system enabling secure on-device LLM inference using ARM TrustZone, but TrustZone was never designed for multi-gigabyte ML models, creating fundamental architectural tension between security and capability. LLMs embedded in mobile apps risk exposing sensitive user data (messages, photos, health records, location) through model interactions.

### Regulatory Pressure Is Increasing

The EU AI Act now mandates adversarial-robustness certification for high-risk edge AI applications (e.g., energy distribution, autonomous vehicles). The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) applies to edge AI hardware and software. IEEE P2938 defines an incremental learning protocol with embedded differential-privacy mechanisms, achieving over 70% reduction in gradient-parameter transmission during edge-model updates, with large-scale pilots confirming scalability across millions of endpoints.

| **4.8.1** | Verify that edge AI devices authenticate to central infrastructure using mutual authentication with certificate validation (e.g., mutual TLS). | 1 | D/V | Rogue devices impersonating legitimate edge AI nodes to receive model updates, exfiltrate data, or inject poisoned model updates. | **4.8.2** | Verify that models deployed to edge or mobile devices are cryptographically signed during packaging, and that the on-device runtime validates these signatures or checksums before loading or inference; unverified or altered models must be rejected. | 1 | D/V | Model tampering during distribution (man-in-the-middle replacing model files). Modified models that produce incorrect or biased results. Backdoored models deployed to edge devices via compromised update channels. Fleet-wide compromise via malicious OTA model updates -- Trend Micro (2025) documents attacks where poisoned parameters are injected through over-the-air update mechanisms, affecting entire device fleets simultaneously. | Verify model packaging includes digital signature (code signing certificate or custom signing scheme). Check that the on-device runtime validates signatures before model loading. Test with a modified model file and confirm it is rejected. Verify signature validation cannot be bypassed by downgrading to an unsigned model format. For on-device LLMs (multi-GB models), verify that signature validation covers all components including adapter/LoRA weights and tokenizer files, not just the base model. | Model signing should cover the complete model artifact: weights, configuration, tokenizer, and any custom operators. For mobile apps, platform code signing (Android APK signing, iOS code signing) provides a baseline but does not cover dynamically downloaded model files. Consider using content integrity verification (hash trees/Merkle trees) for large model files to enable partial verification. The Advantech-SecEdge platform (announced at GTC 2026) demonstrates CRA-compliant model integrity verification for NVIDIA Jetson, ensuring only trusted, verified models execute in industrial applications -- this is an emerging reference architecture for edge model signing. SBOMs for model artifacts (tracking framework version, training lineage, and operator dependencies) complement cryptographic signing. |
| **4.8.3** | Verify that edge devices implement secure boot with verified signatures and rollback protection to prevent firmware downgrade attacks. | 2 | D/V | Firmware downgrade to a version with known vulnerabilities. Persistent rootkit installed by replacing the boot chain. Physical attacker modifying boot firmware to bypass security controls. NVIDIA Jetson security bulletins (July and October 2025) disclosed multiple firmware vulnerabilities enabling RCE and data theft on Jetson Orin, IGX Orin, and Xavier devices -- rollback protection is critical to prevent reversion to these vulnerable firmware versions. | Verify secure boot chain from hardware root of trust through bootloader, OS, and application. Confirm anti-rollback counters prevent firmware downgrade. Test downgrade attempt and verify rejection. Check that all boot chain components are signed by authorized keys. For agentic edge AI systems (autonomous vehicles, industrial robots), verify that the secure boot chain extends to accelerator/NPU firmware as well as perception subsystem firmware (camera, LiDAR controllers). | Secure boot is well-established for x86 (UEFI Secure Boot) and ARM (ARM Trusted Firmware). Edge AI devices (NVIDIA Jetson, Google Coral, Intel NUC) have varying secure boot support. NVIDIA Jetson supports fuse-based secure boot with anti-rollback. Raspberry Pi-based edge AI deployments have limited secure boot options. Consider the full boot chain including GPU/accelerator firmware. The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) now requires demonstrated secure boot and update integrity for edge AI hardware sold in the EU -- Advantech and SecEdge showcased CRA-ready Jetson solutions at GTC 2026. |
| **4.8.4** | Verify that mobile or edge inference applications implement platform-level anti-tampering protections (e.g., code signing, verified boot, runtime integrity checks) that detect and block modified binaries, repackaged applications, or attached instrumentation frameworks. | 2 | D/V | Application repackaging with modified inference logic (returning manipulated results). Attacker attaching debuggers (Frida, lldb) to inspect model weights in memory. Instrumentation frameworks hooking into inference calls to intercept inputs/outputs. Documented real-world attack: researchers used Frida to intercept and capture decrypted TFLite models at runtime from production mobile applications, bypassing all static encryption protections. This demonstrates that anti-tampering is not optional for apps shipping AI models. | Verify code signing is enforced (Android: V2+ signing, iOS: mandatory). Check for runtime integrity mechanisms: root/jailbreak detection, debugger detection, hooking framework detection (Frida, Xposed, Substrate). Test with instrumentation tools (attach Frida to the running app) and verify detection and termination. For on-device LLMs, verify that anti-tampering covers the model loading pathway specifically, not just the application binary. | Anti-tampering is a defense-in-depth measure, not a guarantee -- determined attackers can bypass runtime checks. Layered approach recommended: code signing (baseline), root/jailbreak detection (detect hostile environment), integrity checks (detect modification), obfuscation (increase effort). Commercial solutions: Google Play Integrity API, Apple DeviceCheck/App Attest, third-party RASP tools (Guardsquare, Zimperium). NowSecure (2025) analysis of mobile AI apps found that most ship on-device models completely unprotected, with no runtime integrity checks on the inference pathway. The OWASP Mobile Application Security project provides testing methodology for these controls. |
| **4.8.5** | Verify that distributed AI coordination uses Byzantine fault-tolerant consensus mechanisms with participant validation and malicious node detection. | 3 | D/V | Poisoning attacks in federated learning where malicious participants submit crafted model updates to corrupt the global model. Sybil attacks where an attacker creates multiple fake participants to amplify poisoning. Free-rider attacks where participants submit minimal updates while benefiting from the global model. PoisonedFL (CVPR 2025) demonstrated an attack that defeats eight state-of-the-art FL defenses simultaneously by maintaining consistent poisoning direction across rounds, causing prior defenses' filtering to fail because the attack does not self-cancel like earlier approaches. | Verify BFT consensus protocol handles up to f Byzantine faults among 3f+1 participants. Check participant validation (identity verification, contribution quality scoring). Test with simulated malicious participants submitting poisoned updates and verify detection/exclusion. Evaluate against multi-round consistency attacks (PoisonedFL-style) that evade per-round anomaly detection. Verify that the aggregation mechanism remains effective when up to 70% of participants are malicious (the threshold demonstrated by FedMP). | BFT consensus for federated learning is an active research area. Practical approaches include: robust aggregation (trimmed mean, Krum, FoolsGold), anomaly detection on model updates, and reputation systems. Full BFT consensus adds significant communication overhead (O(n^2) messages). For most federated learning deployments, robust aggregation with anomaly detection is more practical than formal BFT consensus. **2025--2026 advances**: FedMP (multi-pronged defense) detects anomalous variations in both magnitude and direction of model updates across communication rounds and significantly outperforms prior defenses even with 70% malicious clients. The divide-and-conquer (DnC) algorithm is 2.5--12x more resilient than prior Byzantine-robust aggregation. Neuron-centric FL defense (NC-FLD) uses dynamic neuron selection to make it harder for adaptive adversaries to determine which parameters to manipulate. The IACR published a comprehensive survey of adaptive adversaries in Byzantine-robust FL (2025), noting that static defenses are increasingly insufficient against adversaries that adapt their strategy based on observed aggregation behavior. |
| **4.8.6** | Verify that edge-to-cloud communication supports bandwidth throttling, data compression, and secure offline operation with encrypted local storage. | 3 | D/V | Bandwidth exhaustion attacks consuming expensive cellular or satellite connectivity. Data loss during extended offline periods. Unencrypted local storage exposing inference data or cached model updates when device is physically compromised. RF jamming attacks forcing prolonged offline operation to trigger degraded security modes. | Verify bandwidth management policies are configured (rate limiting, compression). Test offline operation: disconnect device and confirm inference continues with cached model. Verify local storage encryption (device-level FDE or application-level encryption). Check that queued data is encrypted and integrity-protected during offline periods. Verify that security posture does not degrade during offline operation (no fallback to weaker authentication or unencrypted storage). | Edge AI devices in industrial/military/remote settings may operate offline for extended periods. Local storage must protect: cached model weights, inference results queue, local training data (for federated learning), and device configuration. Consider storage encryption key management -- keys derived from network authentication cannot be used during offline operation. Hardware-backed key storage (TPM, secure enclave) enables offline encryption without network dependency. IEEE P2938 defines protocols for incremental learning with embedded differential privacy that reduce gradient-parameter transmission by over 70%, directly addressing bandwidth constraints for federated edge deployments. For agentic edge AI systems (autonomous vehicles, drones), watchdog timers and failsafe mechanisms should force graceful degradation rather than unsafe operation when connectivity is lost. |
| **4.8.7** | Verify that on-device inference runtimes enforce process, memory, and file access isolation to prevent model dumping, debugging, or extraction of intermediate embeddings and activations. | 3 | D/V | Model extraction by dumping process memory containing model weights. Intermediate activation extraction revealing sensitive information about training data. Debugging-based extraction of model architecture and parameters. CVE-2025-23270 demonstrated that EM side-channel analysis can extract neural network weights from NVIDIA Jetson chips during inference without any software access -- purely passive physical observation. DMA attacks can read model weights directly from device memory via exposed bus interfaces. | Verify inference runtime runs in an isolated process with restricted debugging (ptrace disabled). Check that memory regions containing model weights are marked as non-dumpable. Verify file access is restricted to prevent reading model files from the filesystem. Test with memory dump tools and confirm model weights are not recoverable. For high-value models, verify that TVLA (Test Vector Leakage Assessment) testing has been performed to detect side-channel leakage during inference. | Full protection against model extraction on a device the attacker physically controls is extremely difficult. This is a defense-in-depth measure. Techniques include: disabling core dumps, using `prctl(PR_SET_DUMPABLE, 0)`, memory encryption (ARM MTE, pointer authentication), and TEE-based inference (OP-TEE, ARM TrustZone). On Android, consider running inference in a separate isolated process with restricted SELinux policy. **2025--2026 developments**: TZ-LLM is the first system enabling secure on-device LLM inference using ARM TrustZone, but faces fundamental architectural tension because TrustZone was never designed for multi-gigabyte ML models. Dell's edge AI security guidance (2025) emphasizes that isolation must be designed in from the start rather than bolted on -- the model must be shielded from everything else running on the device using hardware-enforced environments. For EM side-channel mitigation, constant-time inference execution and EM shielding are emerging countermeasures but add cost and complexity. |
| **4.8.8** | Verify that model weights and sensitive parameters stored locally are encrypted using hardware-backed key stores or secure enclaves (e.g., Android Keystore, iOS Secure Enclave, TPM/TEE), with keys inaccessible to user space. | 3 | D/V | Physical extraction of model weights from device storage (flash memory, SD card). Software-based key extraction from application memory. Cloning attacks where model is copied from one device to another. | Verify encryption keys are stored in hardware-backed keystore (Android Keystore with StrongBox, iOS Secure Enclave, TPM 2.0). Confirm keys are marked as non-exportable. Check that decryption requires device authentication (biometric or PIN). Test that encrypted model files are not decryptable on a different device. For on-device LLMs (multi-GB), verify that key derivation and decryption throughput are sufficient for acceptable model load times. | Hardware-backed encryption binds model weights to a specific device, preventing model copying. Key consideration: model loading performance -- decryption of large model files (hundreds of MB) from hardware-backed keys may have throughput limitations. Consider encrypting only the most sensitive layers/components rather than the entire model file. AES-256-GCM via hardware accelerator typically achieves 1-4 GB/s on modern mobile SoCs, which is adequate for most edge model sizes. **TEE platform landscape (2025--2026)**: ARM TrustZone remains dominant on mobile/edge (billions of deployed devices). Apple Secure Enclave provides hardware-isolated cryptographic processing on all Apple Silicon. Intel SGX is deprecated on consumer CPUs (since 2021) but remains available on Xeon server processors. AWS Nitro Enclaves provide isolated VMs for cloud-edge hybrid deployments. Gartner predicts that by 2026, 50% of large organizations will adopt privacy-enhancing computation (including TEEs) for processing data in untrusted environments. The lack of standardization across TEE platforms remains a significant barrier to portable edge AI security. |
| **4.8.9** | Verify that models packaged within mobile, IoT, or embedded applications are encrypted or obfuscated at rest, and decrypted only inside a trusted runtime or secure enclave, preventing direct extraction from the app package or filesystem. | 3 | D/V | Model extraction from APK/IPA files by unpacking the application bundle. Filesystem-level extraction of model files from rooted/jailbroken devices. Competitive intelligence through reverse engineering of proprietary models. Demonstrated attack: researchers extracted encrypted TFLite models from the Google Photos Android app by using Frida to intercept decryption at runtime, proving that static encryption alone is insufficient without runtime anti-tampering (see 4.8.4). | Verify model files within the application package are encrypted (not just compressed). Check that decryption occurs in memory within a secure context, not to a temporary file. Test by extracting the APK/IPA and attempting to load the model file -- confirm it is not usable. Verify model format is not trivially identifiable (e.g., not a plain TFLite or ONNX file). Combine with 4.8.4 anti-tampering to prevent runtime interception of decrypted models. | Common mobile ML frameworks (TFLite, Core ML, ONNX Runtime) load models from plaintext files by default. Custom model loading with decryption is required. Solutions: custom model encryption with application-specific keys, TFLite model encryption (experimental), and ONNX Runtime custom model loading. Obfuscation alone (renaming files, custom formats) provides minimal security -- encryption is necessary. Consider that determined attackers can extract decrypted models from memory even with encrypted-at-rest protections. **2025--2026 context**: On-device LLMs (1--8B parameter models on mobile) dramatically increase the IP value at risk from extraction. Apple's on-device foundation models (updated 2025) use Core ML with platform-level protections, but third-party apps shipping custom models on iOS/Android remain largely unprotected. Homomorphic encryption enables inference on encrypted data but imposes a 5--10x latency penalty, rendering it impractical for real-time edge applications. The practical defense stack remains: encryption at rest + runtime anti-tampering + TEE-based decryption where hardware supports it. |

## Open Research Questions

- [ ] How effective are current anti-tampering techniques (obfuscation, integrity checks) against determined attackers with physical access to edge AI devices? (Frida-based runtime extraction from production apps demonstrates that static encryption alone is insufficient -- what combination of runtime protections raises the bar meaningfully?)
- [ ] What are the practical tradeoffs between model protection strength and inference latency on resource-constrained edge devices? (TZ-LLM shows feasibility of TrustZone-based LLM inference, but TrustZone's memory constraints create fundamental tension with multi-GB models.)
- [ ] How can federated learning aggregation be made robust against adaptive adversaries that modify their strategy based on observed aggregation behavior? (PoisonedFL defeats eight defenses; FedMP and NC-FLD show promise but the arms race between attack and defense continues.)
- [ ] What is the minimum hardware security capability (TPM, TEE, secure enclave) needed to meaningfully protect model weights on edge devices? (The lack of standardization across TEE platforms -- TrustZone, Secure Enclave, SGX, Nitro Enclaves -- inhibits portable solutions.)
- [ ] How should model update delivery be secured for edge devices with intermittent connectivity and limited bandwidth? (IEEE P2938 addresses bandwidth reduction; CRA compliance adds integrity requirements.)
- [ ] Can EM side-channel attacks (CVE-2025-23270 class) be mitigated on commodity edge AI hardware without prohibitive cost or performance overhead? What role does TVLA testing play in edge AI security validation?
- [ ] How should on-device LLM security differ from traditional edge classifier security, given the dramatically larger model sizes, higher IP value, and richer interaction surfaces (prompt injection, data exfiltration through model interactions)?
- [ ] What security architecture is appropriate for agentic edge AI systems (autonomous vehicles, robots) where perception-layer attacks (sensor spoofing, adversarial physical objects) can cause physical harm?
