# C4.8 Edge & Distributed AI Security

[Back to C04 Index](C04-Infrastructure.md)

## Purpose

Secure distributed AI deployments including edge computing, federated learning, and multi-site architectures. Edge and mobile AI deployments operate in fundamentally hostile environments -- devices are physically accessible to attackers, network connectivity is intermittent, compute resources are constrained, and models deployed to devices can be extracted, reverse-engineered, or tampered with. This section addresses the unique security challenges of running AI inference on devices outside the controlled data center perimeter.

---

## Threat Landscape (2024--2026)

The edge AI attack surface has expanded dramatically as on-device inference moves from simple classifiers to full LLMs running locally on phones, vehicles, robots, and industrial controllers. Key developments include:

### Model Extraction Is Now Practical at Scale

Researchers have demonstrated practical model extraction from production mobile applications. In documented attacks against apps using TensorFlow Lite, attackers used dynamic instrumentation (Frida) to intercept decrypted TFLite models at runtime, bypassing static encryption protections entirely. Studies show most mobile apps today leave their on-device models completely unprotected. A connected-vehicle platform suffered an API abuse attack that exfiltrated core autonomous-driving model parameters, resulting in direct economic losses of $12.7 million due to intellectual-property compromise.

### Side-Channel Attacks on Edge AI Hardware

CVE-2025-23270 (CVSS 7.1) demonstrated that neural network weights can be extracted from NVIDIA Jetson chips by measuring electromagnetic radiation during inference. This class of side-channel attack -- using power consumption, EM emissions, or timing analysis -- is particularly dangerous for edge devices that lack the physical security controls of data centers. The industry-standard Test Vector Leakage Assessment (TVLA) method uses Welch's t-test to detect statistical leakage in power traces, but most edge deployments do not implement such testing.

### Agentic Edge AI Introduces New Attack Layers

The convergence of autonomous AI agents with edge computing (in robots, autonomous vehicles, drones, and industrial systems) has introduced multi-layer attack surfaces spanning perception (sensor spoofing, camera blinding, ultrasonic audio injection), cognition (adversarial inputs, model poisoning via OTA updates), and actuation (command injection, control-channel hijacking). Adversarial perturbations added to smart-home camera input frames can elevate a detector's false-negative rate from 12% to over 60%. By late 2026, automated AI vulnerability research tools can analyze firmware binaries and generate working exploits against IoT edge devices in hours rather than the weeks previously required by human researchers, dramatically compressing the window between device deployment and exploitation. Agentic edge AI also introduces memory poisoning as a new vector -- corrupting an agent's historical data and learned patterns to degrade decision-making accuracy over time, particularly dangerous in autonomous systems where decisions have physical consequences.

### Federated Learning Remains Vulnerable to Sophisticated Poisoning

Recent research (CVPR 2025, NDSS 2025) demonstrates that attacks like PoisonedFL can defeat eight state-of-the-art FL defenses simultaneously, without requiring knowledge of the defense mechanism or access to genuine training data. The attack maintains consistent poisoning direction across rounds, whereas prior attacks' effects self-cancel. New defenses such as FedMP (multi-pronged anomaly detection on update magnitude and direction) and neuron-centric approaches (NC-FLD) show improved resilience even when 70% of participants are malicious, but the arms race continues. As of early 2026, DAARA (Divergence-Aware Attention for Robust Aggregation) shifts the defensive perspective from global anomaly detection to client-level divergence analysis, adaptively assigning attention weights based on statistical divergence and class-wise consistency -- outperforming Krum, Trimmed Mean, FoolsGold, and RFed by up to 40% in benchmarks on cybersecurity datasets (NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15). FedVoD introduces a dual-voting mechanism for selecting trustworthy updates through similarity-based voting across training rounds, representing the trend toward multi-signal trust assessment rather than single-metric filtering.

### On-Device LLMs Expand the Attack Surface

On-device LLMs (running on mobile SoCs with 8--16 GB RAM) expose significantly more intellectual property than traditional edge classifiers. TZ-LLM is the first system enabling secure on-device LLM inference using ARM TrustZone, reducing time-to-first-token by up to 90.9% compared to naive TEE approaches, but TrustZone was never designed for multi-gigabyte ML models, creating fundamental architectural tension between security and capability. As of March 2026, FlexServe offers a concurrent approach that eliminates TrustZone's contiguous memory allocation requirement entirely, achieving only 4.41% overhead versus unprotected inference and a 24.3x speedup for multi-model agentic workflows -- a meaningful step toward practical secure on-device LLM serving. ARM's Confidential Compute Architecture (CCA), available on Armv9-A processors, introduces Realms as a more flexible alternative to TrustZone, using page-granular MMU-based isolation rather than coarse-grained bus-level protection, though CCA-capable edge silicon is still in early adoption. LLMs embedded in mobile apps risk exposing sensitive user data (messages, photos, health records, location) through model interactions.

### Regulatory Pressure Is Increasing

The EU AI Act now mandates adversarial-robustness certification for high-risk edge AI applications (e.g., energy distribution, autonomous vehicles). The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) applies to edge AI hardware and software. IEEE P2938 defines an incremental learning protocol with embedded differential-privacy mechanisms, achieving over 70% reduction in gradient-parameter transmission during edge-model updates, with large-scale pilots confirming scalability across millions of endpoints.

| **4.8.8** | Verify that model weights and sensitive parameters stored locally are encrypted using hardware-backed key stores or secure enclaves (e.g., Android Keystore, iOS Secure Enclave, TPM/TEE), with keys inaccessible to user space. | 3 | D/V | Physical extraction of model weights from device storage (flash memory, SD card). Software-based key extraction from application memory. For on-device LLMs (multi-GB), verify that key derivation and decryption throughput are sufficient for acceptable model load times. | Hardware-backed encryption binds model weights to a specific device, preventing model copying. Key consideration: model loading performance -- decryption of large model files (hundreds of MB) from hardware-backed keys may have throughput limitations. Consider encrypting only the most sensitive layers/components rather than the entire model file. AES-256-GCM via hardware accelerator typically achieves 1-4 GB/s on modern mobile SoCs, which is adequate for most edge model sizes. **TEE platform landscape (2025--2026)**: ARM TrustZone remains dominant on mobile/edge (billions of deployed devices). ARM CCA (Armv9-A) introduces Realms with page-granular isolation via the Realm Management Extension (RME), extending the circle of trust to accelerators and enabling dynamic memory allocation that TrustZone's coarse-grained TZASC cannot match -- early adopters report keeping 97% of model logic isolated with roughly 25% performance overhead. AWS Nitro Enclaves provide isolated VMs for cloud-edge hybrid deployments. Gartner predicts that by 2026, 50% of large organizations will adopt privacy-enhancing computation (including TEEs) for processing data in untrusted environments. The Advantech-SecEdge SEC TPM integration with AWS IoT Greengrass (GTC 2026) demonstrates a production-ready reference for lifecycle-managed model encryption on NVIDIA Jetson using hardware TPM. The lack of standardization across TEE platforms remains a significant barrier to portable edge AI security. |
| **4.8.9** | Verify that models packaged within mobile, IoT, or embedded applications are encrypted or obfuscated at rest, and decrypted only inside a trusted runtime or secure enclave, preventing direct extraction from the app package or filesystem. | 3 | D/V | Model extraction from APK/IPA files by unpacking the application bundle. Filesystem-level extraction of model files from rooted/jailbroken devices. Competitive intelligence through reverse engineering of proprietary models. Demonstrated attack: researchers extracted encrypted TFLite models from the Google Photos Android app by using Frida to intercept decryption at runtime, proving that static encryption alone is insufficient without runtime anti-tampering (see 4.8.4). | Verify model files within the application package are encrypted (not just compressed). Check that decryption occurs in memory within a secure context, not to a temporary file. Test by extracting the APK/IPA and attempting to load the model file -- confirm it is not usable. Verify model format is not trivially identifiable (e.g., not a plain TFLite or ONNX file). Obfuscation alone (renaming files, custom formats) provides minimal security -- encryption is necessary. Consider that determined attackers can extract decrypted models from memory even with encrypted-at-rest protections. **2025--2026 context**: On-device LLMs (1--8B parameter models on mobile) dramatically increase the IP value at risk from extraction. The AI model watermarking market is projected to grow from $0.33 billion (2024) to $1.17 billion by 2029, reflecting industry investment in IP protection -- model watermarking embeds detectable ownership markers into weights, while model fingerprinting generates unique verification samples without modifying the model. Apple's on-device foundation models (updated 2025) use Core ML with platform-level protections, but third-party apps shipping custom models on iOS/Android remain largely unprotected. Homomorphic encryption enables inference on encrypted data but imposes a 5--10x latency penalty, rendering it impractical for real-time edge applications. The practical defense stack remains: encryption at rest + runtime anti-tampering + TEE-based decryption where hardware supports it. NVIDIA's TensorRT Edge-LLM SDK (announced with Jetson T4000 at GTC 2026) provides a production-oriented LLM runtime for edge GPUs, but security integration with the JetPack 7.1 secure boot and disk encryption stack requires explicit configuration -- it is not secure by default. |

## Open Research Questions

- [ ] How effective are current anti-tampering techniques (obfuscation, integrity checks) against determined attackers with physical access to edge AI devices? (Frida-based runtime extraction from production apps demonstrates that static encryption alone is insufficient -- what combination of runtime protections raises the bar meaningfully?)
- [ ] What are the practical tradeoffs between model protection strength and inference latency on resource-constrained edge devices? (TZ-LLM and FlexServe demonstrate that TEE-based LLM inference is feasible with 4--25% overhead, but multi-model agentic workflows and CCA Realm transitions add complexity that is not yet well characterized.)
- [ ] How can federated learning aggregation be made robust against adaptive adversaries that modify their strategy based on observed aggregation behavior? (PoisonedFL defeats eight defenses; newer approaches like DAARA and FedVoD shift to client-level divergence analysis and dual-voting, but the arms race continues with adversaries adapting to each new defense signal.)
- [ ] What is the minimum hardware security capability (TPM, TEE, secure enclave) needed to meaningfully protect model weights on edge devices? (ARM CCA Realms offer a promising standardization path on Armv9-A, but adoption across edge silicon vendors is uncertain, and the TrustZone/Secure Enclave/SGX/Nitro fragmentation persists for current-generation devices.)
- [ ] How should model update delivery be secured for edge devices with intermittent connectivity and limited bandwidth? (IEEE P2938 addresses bandwidth reduction; CRA compliance adds integrity requirements.)
- [ ] Can EM side-channel attacks (CVE-2025-23270 class) be mitigated on commodity edge AI hardware without prohibitive cost or performance overhead? What role does TVLA testing play in edge AI security validation?
- [ ] How should on-device LLM security differ from traditional edge classifier security, given the dramatically larger model sizes, higher IP value, and richer interaction surfaces (prompt injection, data exfiltration through model interactions)?
- [ ] What security architecture is appropriate for agentic edge AI systems (autonomous vehicles, robots) where perception-layer attacks (sensor spoofing, adversarial physical objects) can cause physical harm?
- [ ] How effective are model watermarking and fingerprinting techniques for detecting unauthorized deployment of extracted edge AI models, and can they survive common post-extraction transformations (pruning, quantization, fine-tuning)?
- [ ] As automated AI vulnerability research tools compress the exploit development timeline from weeks to hours for edge devices, how should patch delivery and secure OTA cadences adapt for devices with intermittent connectivity?
