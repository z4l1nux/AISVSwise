# C4.8 Edge & Distributed AI Security

[Back to C04 Index](C04-Infrastructure.md)

## Purpose

Secure distributed AI deployments including edge computing, federated learning, and multi-site architectures. Edge and mobile AI deployments operate in fundamentally hostile environments -- devices are physically accessible to attackers, network connectivity is intermittent, compute resources are constrained, and models deployed to devices can be extracted, reverse-engineered, or tampered with. This section addresses the unique security challenges of running AI inference on devices outside the controlled data center perimeter.

---

## Threat Landscape (2024--2026)

The edge AI attack surface has expanded dramatically as on-device inference moves from simple classifiers to full LLMs running locally on phones, vehicles, robots, and industrial controllers. As of early 2026, an estimated 80% of AI inference now happens locally on devices rather than in cloud data centers, and the security implications are profound. Nearly 3 billion malicious sessions targeted edge infrastructure in H2 2025 alone (averaging 212 malicious sessions per second across 80+ countries), with VPN appliances, consumer routers, and remote access services absorbing sustained exploitation from both criminal and nation-state actors. CISA responded with Binding Operational Directive 26-02 (February 2026), mandating that federal agencies inventory and remove all unsupported edge devices within 12--18 months -- a recognition that unpatched edge infrastructure has become the primary initial-access vector for persistent threat actors. Key developments include:

### Model Extraction Is Now Practical at Scale

Researchers have demonstrated practical model extraction from production mobile applications. In documented attacks against apps using TensorFlow Lite, attackers used dynamic instrumentation (Frida) to intercept decrypted TFLite models at runtime, bypassing static encryption protections entirely. Studies show most mobile apps today leave their on-device models completely unprotected. A connected-vehicle platform suffered an API abuse attack that exfiltrated core autonomous-driving model parameters, resulting in direct economic losses of $12.7 million due to intellectual-property compromise.

### Side-Channel Attacks on Edge AI Hardware -- and Now Network Traffic

CVE-2025-23270 (CVSS 7.1) demonstrated that neural network weights can be extracted from NVIDIA Jetson chips by measuring electromagnetic radiation during inference. This class of side-channel attack -- using power consumption, EM emissions, or timing analysis -- is particularly dangerous for edge devices that lack the physical security controls of data centers. The industry-standard Test Vector Leakage Assessment (TVLA) method uses Welch's t-test to detect statistical leakage in power traces, but most edge deployments do not implement such testing. A newer class of side-channel threat targets network traffic rather than hardware: Whisper Leak (Microsoft Research, November 2025) demonstrated that encrypted LLM traffic can be analyzed to infer user prompt topics by exploiting packet size and timing patterns in streaming responses. The attack achieved near-perfect classification (>98% AUPRC) across 28 popular LLMs from major providers, with 100% precision in identifying sensitive topics like financial crime discussions. Because TLS preserves the size relationship between plaintext and ciphertext, token-by-token streaming leaks statistical patterns that reveal conversation topics even through encryption. Proposed mitigations (random padding, token batching, packet injection) reduce but do not eliminate the attack's effectiveness. For edge-to-cloud LLM communication, this means that encryption alone is insufficient to protect prompt confidentiality.

### TEE-Based Model Protection Has Fundamental Weaknesses

Research published in early 2026 revealed a systemic vulnerability in partial TEE-shielded LLM inference systems that rely on precomputed noise vectors for performance optimization. These systems precompute a small set of K secret basis vectors at startup and combine them via random coefficients during inference -- confining all "random" noise to a predictable, low-dimensional subspace. Attackers can collect K+delta query outputs with zero-vector inputs, reconstruct the noise subspace via Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization, and then recover secret permutation matrices to extract full model weights. For LLaMA-3 8B, a single layer's secrets can be recovered in approximately 6.2 minutes; the full 405B model requires under two hours per layer. The attack achieves perfect model replicas with identical accuracy. Proposed countermeasures (random subset sampling, increasing K) are ineffective -- this is a fundamental cryptographic flaw rooted in key material reuse, not an implementation bug. This finding challenges the viability of any partial TEE approach that uses static precomputation for performance.

### Supply Chain Compromise: Factory-Infected Devices at Scale

The BadBox 2.0 botnet (disrupted mid-2025, Google lawsuit filed July 2025) demonstrated that supply chain compromise of edge devices can operate at massive scale. Over 10 million budget Android-based IoT devices -- smart TVs, streaming boxes, tablets, and digital projectors -- were shipped with malware pre-installed in firmware before leaving factories, primarily in Asia. Infected devices were enrolled into a residential proxy botnet spanning 222 countries, used for ad fraud, credential theft, and DDoS operations. The Aisuru/TurboMirai botnet variant achieved 20+ Tbps DDoS capability from recruited IoT endpoints. BadBox 2.0 specifically targeted uncertified Android Open Source Project (AOSP) devices rather than Play Protect-certified hardware, highlighting that device certification and hardware attestation are not optional for any edge AI deployment handling sensitive inference workloads.

### Edge Device Patching Crisis

As of early 2026, the edge device ecosystem faces a systemic patching crisis. Many edge AI systems run proprietary Board Support Packages with custom kernels that become unmaintainable when original build environments disappear. Industry analysis identifies "frozen devices" -- systems where legacy kernel patches no longer exist -- as a growing fleet-wide vulnerability. The EU Cyber Resilience Act will classify inability to patch as a compliance violation, but the practical challenge remains: AI workloads at the edge cannot tolerate traditional patch-and-reboot cycles, with even 10-minute reboots creating dangerous operational gaps for autonomous systems. Organizations are moving toward containerized deployments enabling hot-swap model updates and A/B redundancy (blue-green deployment) to enable zero-downtime patching. A separate concern is security testing parity -- teams commonly disable Secure Boot, disk encryption, and SELinux in CI/CD testing for automation convenience, creating dangerous divergence from production security posture.

### Agentic Edge AI Introduces New Attack Layers

The convergence of autonomous AI agents with edge computing (in robots, autonomous vehicles, drones, and industrial systems) has introduced multi-layer attack surfaces spanning perception (sensor spoofing, camera blinding, ultrasonic audio injection), cognition (adversarial inputs, model poisoning via OTA updates), and actuation (command injection, control-channel hijacking). Adversarial perturbations added to smart-home camera input frames can elevate a detector's false-negative rate from 12% to over 60%. By late 2026, automated AI vulnerability research tools can analyze firmware binaries and generate working exploits against IoT edge devices in hours rather than the weeks previously required by human researchers, dramatically compressing the window between device deployment and exploitation. Agentic edge AI also introduces memory poisoning as a new vector -- corrupting an agent's historical data and learned patterns to degrade decision-making accuracy over time, particularly dangerous in autonomous systems where decisions have physical consequences.

### Ransomware Targeting Connected Vehicles and Smart Mobility

Ransomware attacks on automotive and smart mobility systems doubled in 2025, with 44% of the 494 publicly reported automotive cybersecurity incidents involving ransomware -- more than double the 2024 volume. Notably, 92% of automotive cyber attacks were conducted remotely with no physical proximity required, and 67% of incidents involved telematics and cloud-connected systems with APIs as the primary attack vector. In a mid-2025 incident, attackers accessed vehicle command systems through a companion app, locked owners out, took remote control of ignition and door locks, and demanded ransom. A major European OEM attack paralyzed production for several weeks, impacting suppliers and regional GDP. The broader IoT/edge ransomware landscape is projected to cause $57 billion in damages globally in 2026.

### Federated Learning Remains Vulnerable to Sophisticated Poisoning

Recent research (CVPR 2025, NDSS 2025) demonstrates that attacks like PoisonedFL can defeat eight state-of-the-art FL defenses simultaneously, without requiring knowledge of the defense mechanism or access to genuine training data. The attack maintains consistent poisoning direction across rounds, whereas prior attacks' effects self-cancel. New defenses such as FedMP (multi-pronged anomaly detection on update magnitude and direction) and neuron-centric approaches (NC-FLD) show improved resilience even when 70% of participants are malicious, but the arms race continues. As of early 2026, DAARA (Divergence-Aware Attention for Robust Aggregation) shifts the defensive perspective from global anomaly detection to client-level divergence analysis, adaptively assigning attention weights based on statistical divergence and class-wise consistency -- outperforming Krum, Trimmed Mean, FoolsGold, and RFed by up to 40% in benchmarks on cybersecurity datasets (NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15). FedVoD introduces a dual-voting mechanism for selecting trustworthy updates through similarity-based voting across training rounds, representing the trend toward multi-signal trust assessment rather than single-metric filtering. FedRDF (IEEE TETC, 2025) takes a fundamentally different approach by projecting client-generated weights into the frequency domain using Fourier Transform, selecting updates exhibiting the highest frequency component -- this spectral analysis can detect attack patterns that spatial statistical methods (median, trimmed mean, Krum) miss entirely, and crucially requires no prior knowledge of the number of attackers. A dual-defense line of work combining FHE-based secure aggregation with encrypted model similarity detection addresses the tension between privacy-preserving aggregation and poisoning defense, since standard secure aggregation obscures the very update patterns that anomaly detection needs to see.

### On-Device LLMs Expand the Attack Surface

On-device LLMs (running on mobile SoCs with 8--16 GB RAM) expose significantly more intellectual property than traditional edge classifiers. As of March 2026, on-device LLMs are no longer experimental -- they are embedded in core mobile applications with access to personal data (messages, photos, health records, location), and security scanning has identified over 175,000 exposed Ollama LLM inference servers globally, illustrating the scale of the exposed attack surface. TZ-LLM is the first system enabling secure on-device LLM inference using ARM TrustZone, reducing time-to-first-token by up to 90.9% compared to naive TEE approaches, but TrustZone was never designed for multi-gigabyte ML models, creating fundamental architectural tension between security and capability. As of March 2026, FlexServe offers a concurrent approach that eliminates TrustZone's contiguous memory allocation requirement entirely, achieving only 4.41% overhead versus unprotected inference and a 24.3x speedup for multi-model agentic workflows -- a meaningful step toward practical secure on-device LLM serving. ARM's Confidential Compute Architecture (CCA), available on Armv9-A processors, introduces Realms as a more flexible alternative to TrustZone, using page-granular MMU-based isolation rather than coarse-grained bus-level protection, though CCA-capable edge silicon is still in early adoption. LLMs embedded in mobile apps risk exposing sensitive user data (messages, photos, health records, location) through model interactions. LoRO (NeurIPS 2025) offers a different strategy: rather than isolating the entire model inside a TEE, it uses low-rank factors to generate dense masks that completely obfuscate model parameters in the untrusted environment, reducing complexity from O(n^3) to O(n^2) and achieving only 1.49x latency overhead compared to 112x for prior secure inference approaches -- it requires no model retraining or modification, downgrading extractable models to black-box level (0.94x membership inference accuracy). However, the precomputed-noise vulnerability described above applies to any system that caches secret basis vectors for performance, so implementers must carefully evaluate whether their TEE strategy relies on static precomputation patterns.

### Model Compression Introduces Security Brittleness

The push toward smaller on-device models through quantization (INT4, INT2/INT3), pruning, and knowledge distillation enables deployment on constrained hardware but introduces security trade-offs that are often overlooked. Compressed models can become more brittle, with reduced tolerance for noisy or adversarial inputs. As of March 2026, sub-1B parameter models (125M--1.7B range) are viable for practical tasks on mobile devices, and flagship efficient models like Llama 3.2 (1B/3B), Gemma 3 (270M--27B), and Phi-4 (3.8B--14B) are widely deployed. Frameworks like Meta's ExecuTorch 1.0 (50KB footprint, 12+ hardware backends), llama.cpp (GGUF format for CPU inference), Apple's MLX, and MLC-LLM enable cross-platform deployment. However, aggressive quantization using techniques like AWQ/GPTQ at 4-bit or emerging 2-bit approaches (BitNet) reduces the representational capacity that models need to resist adversarial perturbations. Outlier-handling methods (SmoothQuant, SpinQuant) partially address precision-related robustness loss but add complexity. Organizations deploying compressed models at the edge should evaluate adversarial robustness before and after compression, as a model that resists adversarial examples at FP16 may fail at INT4.

### RISC-V TEEs Emerge as Open Alternative for Edge AI Security

The RISC-V ecosystem is gaining a confidential computing capability relevant to edge AI. GATOR-V (2025) is the first production-grade, upstream-ready TEE for RISC-V, providing hardware-enforced isolation with a standards-compliant trusted OS architecture. Released as open source, GATOR-V demonstrated strong security guarantees with minimal performance overhead across multiple RISC-V platforms. AP-TEE provides a RISC-V counterpart to AMD SEV, Intel TDX, and ARM CCA for confidential VMs. For edge AI, this means that RISC-V-based inference platforms (increasingly common in cost-sensitive IoT deployments) are no longer limited to software-only isolation. By 2026, the IoT semiconductor landscape is shifting toward RISC-V and chiplet-based designs, with security-by-design and hardware roots of trust determining which silicon vendors make procurement shortlists. Meanwhile, ARM CCA Realms continue to mature: the CAEC system (accepted at EuroS&P 2026) extends ARM CCA firmware to support Confidential Shared Memory (CSM) between confidential VMs, achieving up to 209x reduction in CPU cycles for inter-realm communication compared to encryption-based mechanisms -- a critical capability for multi-model agentic workflows where isolated models need to exchange inference results securely.

### Regulatory Pressure Is Increasing

The EU AI Act now mandates adversarial-robustness certification for high-risk edge AI applications (e.g., energy distribution, autonomous vehicles), with full compliance for high-risk systems required by August 2027 and most organizational obligations active by August 2026. The EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) applies to edge AI hardware and software; from 11 September 2026, manufacturers will be legally required to report actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe cybersecurity incidents affecting products with digital elements -- this is the first operational CRA milestone. The CRA mandates security-by-design, software integrity protection, vulnerability disclosure procedures, and security update provision throughout the product lifecycle, making the edge patching crisis described above a direct compliance liability. IEEE P2938 defines an incremental learning protocol with embedded differential-privacy mechanisms, achieving over 70% reduction in gradient-parameter transmission during edge-model updates, with large-scale pilots confirming scalability across millions of endpoints. Kudelski Labs presented at Embedded World 2026 on post-quantum cryptography integration for edge AI device lifecycle security, reflecting growing industry awareness that current cryptographic protections for edge model distribution may not survive quantum computing advances.

AES-256-GCM via hardware accelerator typically achieves 1-4 GB/s on modern mobile SoCs, which is adequate for most edge model sizes. **TEE platform landscape (2025--2026)**: ARM TrustZone remains dominant on mobile/edge (billions of deployed devices). ARM CCA (Armv9-A) introduces Realms with page-granular isolation via the Realm Management Extension (RME), extending the circle of trust to accelerators and enabling dynamic memory allocation that TrustZone's coarse-grained TZASC cannot match -- early adopters report keeping 97% of model logic isolated with roughly 25% performance overhead. Apple Secure Enclave provides hardware-isolated cryptographic processing on all Apple Silicon. Intel SGX is deprecated on consumer CPUs (since 2021) but remains available on Xeon server processors. AWS Nitro Enclaves provide isolated VMs for cloud-edge hybrid deployments. Gartner predicts that by 2026, 50% of large organizations will adopt privacy-enhancing computation (including TEEs) for processing data in untrusted environments. The Advantech-SecEdge SEC TPM integration with AWS IoT Greengrass (GTC 2026) demonstrates a production-ready reference for lifecycle-managed model encryption on NVIDIA Jetson using hardware TPM. The RISC-V ecosystem now has GATOR-V as its first production-grade TEE, and AP-TEE provides confidential VM support comparable to AMD SEV/Intel TDX/ARM CCA, closing a critical gap for RISC-V-based edge AI platforms. CAEC (EuroS&P 2026) extends ARM CCA with Confidential Shared Memory between realms, achieving 209x CPU cycle reduction for inter-CVM communication -- important for multi-model agentic edge workflows requiring secure inter-model data exchange. The lack of standardization across TEE platforms remains a significant barrier to portable edge AI security, though the convergence toward hardware-backed confidential computing across ARM, RISC-V, and x86 architectures is narrowing the gap. |
| **4.8.9** | Verify that models packaged within mobile, IoT, or embedded applications are encrypted or obfuscated at rest, and decrypted only inside a trusted runtime or secure enclave, preventing direct extraction from the app package or filesystem. | 3 | D/V | Model extraction from APK/IPA files by unpacking the application bundle. Filesystem-level extraction of model files from rooted/jailbroken devices. Competitive intelligence through reverse engineering of proprietary models. Demonstrated attack: researchers extracted encrypted TFLite models from the Google Photos Android app by using Frida to intercept decryption at runtime, proving that static encryption alone is insufficient without runtime anti-tampering (see 4.8.4). | Verify model files within the application package are encrypted (not just compressed). Check that decryption occurs in memory within a secure context, not to a temporary file. Test by extracting the APK/IPA and attempting to load the model file -- confirm it is not usable. Verify model format is not trivially identifiable (e.g., not a plain TFLite or ONNX file). Combine with 4.8.4 anti-tampering to prevent runtime interception of decrypted models. | Common mobile ML frameworks (TFLite, Core ML, ONNX Runtime) load models from plaintext files by default. Custom model loading with decryption is required. Solutions: custom model encryption with application-specific keys, TFLite model encryption (experimental), and ONNX Runtime custom model loading. Obfuscation alone (renaming files, custom formats) provides minimal security -- encryption is necessary. Consider that determined attackers can extract decrypted models from memory even with encrypted-at-rest protections. **2025--2026 context**: On-device LLMs (1--8B parameter models on mobile) dramatically increase the IP value at risk from extraction. The AI model watermarking market is projected to grow from $0.33 billion (2024) to $1.17 billion by 2029, reflecting industry investment in IP protection -- model watermarking embeds detectable ownership markers into weights, while model fingerprinting generates unique verification samples without modifying the model. Apple's on-device foundation models (updated 2025) use Core ML with platform-level protections, but third-party apps shipping custom models on iOS/Android remain largely unprotected. Homomorphic encryption enables inference on encrypted data but imposes a 5--10x latency penalty, rendering it impractical for real-time edge applications. The practical defense stack remains: encryption at rest + runtime anti-tampering + TEE-based decryption where hardware supports it. NVIDIA's TensorRT Edge-LLM SDK (announced with Jetson T4000 at GTC 2026) provides a production-oriented LLM runtime for edge GPUs, but security integration with the JetPack 7.1 secure boot and disk encryption stack requires explicit configuration -- it is not secure by default. On-device LLMs also introduce prompt injection as an edge-specific risk: Android's developer security guidance (2026) warns that on-device LLM components must operate with minimum permissions and never be granted sensitive Android permissions (READ_CONTACTS, ACCESS_FINE_LOCATION) for model consumption unless absolutely critical, as prompt injection can cause local LLMs to exfiltrate user data through crafted output channels. Data from external sources processed by on-device models should be clearly marked as untrusted to prevent indirect prompt injection. As of March 2026, the on-device LLM deployment ecosystem has matured substantially: Meta's ExecuTorch 1.0 (50KB footprint, 12+ hardware backends), llama.cpp (GGUF format), Apple MLX, and MLC-LLM provide production-ready inference runtimes. Sub-1B parameter models (Llama 3.2 1B/3B, Gemma 3 270M--27B, Phi-4 3.8B--14B) are now practical on mobile hardware with <4GB available RAM. However, aggressive model compression (INT4/INT2 quantization, structured pruning) can reduce adversarial robustness -- models that resist adversarial examples at FP16 may fail after quantization, so security testing must be performed on the compressed artifact, not only the original model. |

## Open Research Questions

- [ ] How effective are current anti-tampering techniques (obfuscation, integrity checks) against determined attackers with physical access to edge AI devices? (Frida-based runtime extraction from production apps demonstrates that static encryption alone is insufficient -- what combination of runtime protections raises the bar meaningfully?)
- [ ] What are the practical tradeoffs between model protection strength and inference latency on resource-constrained edge devices? (TZ-LLM and FlexServe demonstrate that TEE-based LLM inference is feasible with 4--25% overhead, but multi-model agentic workflows and CCA Realm transitions add complexity that is not yet well characterized.)
- [ ] How can federated learning aggregation be made robust against adaptive adversaries that modify their strategy based on observed aggregation behavior? (PoisonedFL defeats eight defenses; newer approaches like DAARA and FedVoD shift to client-level divergence analysis and dual-voting, but the arms race continues with adversaries adapting to each new defense signal.)
- [ ] What is the minimum hardware security capability (TPM, TEE, secure enclave) needed to meaningfully protect model weights on edge devices? (ARM CCA Realms offer a promising standardization path on Armv9-A, but adoption across edge silicon vendors is uncertain, and the TrustZone/Secure Enclave/SGX/Nitro fragmentation persists for current-generation devices.)
- [ ] How should model update delivery be secured for edge devices with intermittent connectivity and limited bandwidth? (IEEE P2938 addresses bandwidth reduction; CRA compliance adds integrity requirements.)
- [ ] Can EM side-channel attacks (CVE-2025-23270 class) be mitigated on commodity edge AI hardware without prohibitive cost or performance overhead? What role does TVLA testing play in edge AI security validation?
- [ ] How should on-device LLM security differ from traditional edge classifier security, given the dramatically larger model sizes, higher IP value, and richer interaction surfaces (prompt injection, data exfiltration through model interactions)?
- [ ] What security architecture is appropriate for agentic edge AI systems (autonomous vehicles, robots) where perception-layer attacks (sensor spoofing, adversarial physical objects) can cause physical harm?
- [ ] How effective are model watermarking and fingerprinting techniques for detecting unauthorized deployment of extracted edge AI models, and can they survive common post-extraction transformations (pruning, quantization, fine-tuning)?
- [ ] As automated AI vulnerability research tools compress the exploit development timeline from weeks to hours for edge devices, how should patch delivery and secure OTA cadences adapt for devices with intermittent connectivity?
- [ ] Given the demonstrated fundamental vulnerability in partial TEE-shielded inference using precomputed noise bases, can any performance-optimized TEE strategy provide meaningful model confidentiality, or is full model encapsulation within the TEE the only viable path despite its performance costs?
- [ ] How should the tension between secure aggregation (hiding updates for privacy) and poisoning defense (inspecting updates for integrity) be resolved in federated learning systems? Do FHE-based dual-defense approaches scale to production deployments?
- [ ] What post-quantum cryptographic primitives are practical for edge AI model distribution and device authentication, given the constrained compute and power budgets of edge hardware? (Corvex's use of ML-KEM/Kyber-768 for GPU-level key exchange demonstrates feasibility at the data center edge, but mobile and IoT endpoints face tighter constraints.)
- [ ] How should prompt injection defenses for on-device LLMs differ from cloud-hosted LLMs, given that local models may have direct access to device sensors, contacts, location, and local files without the network-level monitoring available in cloud deployments?
- [ ] As ransomware operators increasingly target connected vehicles and smart mobility systems (44% of automotive cyber incidents in 2025), what security architectures can ensure that edge AI inference continues safely during an active ransomware attack on telematics infrastructure?
- [ ] How does aggressive model compression (INT4/INT2 quantization, structured pruning) affect adversarial robustness of edge-deployed models, and should security testing be mandatory on the compressed artifact rather than the original model?
- [ ] Can RISC-V TEE implementations (GATOR-V, AP-TEE) achieve security parity with ARM TrustZone/CCA for edge AI workloads, and what are the implications of open-source TEE designs for auditability versus proprietary attack surface reduction?
- [ ] How should edge AI deployments defend against traffic analysis side channels (Whisper Leak class) when models must communicate with cloud infrastructure, given that proposed mitigations (padding, batching, injection) individually provide incomplete protection?
