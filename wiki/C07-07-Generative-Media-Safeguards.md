# C7.7 Generative Media Safeguards

[Back to C07 Index](C07-Model-Behavior)

## Purpose

Generative media safeguards address the unique risks of AI systems that produce images, audio, video, or other non-text media. These systems can create non-consensual intimate imagery, generate deepfakes of real people, produce copyright-infringing content, and manufacture disinformation at scale. Unlike text-based safety filtering, media safeguards must operate across modalities — analyzing both the text prompt that drives generation and the resulting media artifact. This section covers input filtering, consent verification, copyright checking, bypass detection, and provenance marking.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Role | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|:----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **7.7.1** | **Verify that** input filters block prompts requesting explicit or non-consensual synthetic content before the model processes them. | 1 | D/V | **Generation of illegal or harmful imagery.** Without input filtering, users can prompt image/audio/video models to generate child sexual abuse material (CSAM), non-consensual intimate imagery, or graphic violence. Blocking at the prompt level prevents the model from ever generating such content. | Submit prompts with explicit requests for prohibited content categories (CSAM, non-consensual imagery, graphic violence). Verify all are blocked before reaching the model. Test indirect phrasing, euphemisms, and multi-language prompts to assess filter robustness. Confirm block events are logged. | Prompt-level filtering is necessary but not sufficient — adversarial users continuously develop new phrasings to bypass filters. Multi-layer defense (prompt filter + output classifier + human review for edge cases) is more robust. Keyword matching alone is easily evaded; ML-based intent classification is more effective but requires ongoing training data. |
| **7.7.2** | **Verify that** the system refuses to generate media (images/audio) that depicts real people without verified consent. | 2 | D/V | **Non-consensual deepfakes.** AI-generated images and audio depicting real people can be used for fraud (voice cloning for social engineering), defamation, political manipulation, and non-consensual pornography. Without identity verification, the model generates content depicting anyone. | Attempt to generate images or audio of identifiable public figures and private individuals. Verify the system either refuses generation or requires proof of consent. Test with both explicit name references and indirect descriptions ("the current US president"). Verify face detection runs on generated outputs as a secondary check. | Technically challenging. Requires either: (a) celebrity/public figure blocklists with face recognition on outputs, (b) a consent verification workflow, or (c) architectural design that prevents realistic depictions of real individuals. Voice cloning services face the same challenge — the "consent" verification is often trivially bypassable. Current implementations (DALL-E, Midjourney) use blocklists and output classifiers, but coverage is incomplete. |
| **7.7.3** | **Verify that** the system checks generated content for copyright violations before releasing it. | 2 | V | **Copyright infringement.** Generative models trained on copyrighted works can produce outputs that closely resemble copyrighted material (exact reproductions of artworks, near-identical melodies, copyrighted character depictions). Releasing such content exposes the organization to legal liability. | Generate content in styles or subjects likely to produce near-copyrighted outputs (e.g., well-known characters, iconic artworks, famous musical compositions). Verify the system performs similarity checking against a reference database and flags or blocks outputs exceeding a similarity threshold. | This is one of the least mature areas. No production-ready system reliably detects copyright infringement in generated media. Approaches include perceptual hashing against known copyrighted works, image retrieval against training data, and style/content similarity scoring. Legal standards for "substantial similarity" are themselves ambiguous. Current best practice is to log outputs and maintain a takedown process rather than relying solely on automated detection. |
| **7.7.4** | **Verify that** attempts to bypass filters are detected and logged as security events. | 2 | D/V | **Filter evasion and adversarial attacks.** Users attempting to bypass media safety filters (via obfuscation, encoding tricks, multi-step generation, or adversarial prompts) represent a security threat. Detecting and logging bypass attempts enables identification of malicious users and improvement of filters. | Attempt known bypass techniques: character substitution, base64 encoding of prohibited terms, multi-step prompting (generate background separately from foreground), language switching, and adversarial prompt patterns. Verify the system detects these attempts (even if the generation is blocked) and logs them as security events with user identification. | Bypass detection requires pattern analysis beyond simple prompt matching. Behavioral signals include: rapid sequential related prompts, progressive boundary testing, use of known obfuscation patterns, and prompt structures commonly associated with jailbreak attempts. These signals should feed into C7.6 monitoring and may trigger rate limiting per C7.4. |
| **7.7.5** | **Verify that** all generated media includes an invisible watermark or cryptographic signature to prove it was AI-generated. | 3 | D/V | **Disinformation and provenance uncertainty.** Without provenance marking, AI-generated media is indistinguishable from authentic media, enabling deepfake-based disinformation, fraud, and manipulation. Watermarks and signatures provide a technical mechanism for verifying whether content was AI-generated. | Generate media through the system and verify each output contains either: (a) an invisible watermark detectable by the corresponding verification tool, or (b) C2PA-compliant metadata embedding. Test watermark robustness against common transformations (screenshot, compression, cropping, format conversion). Verify a verification tool can confirm AI provenance from the watermarked output. | Two main approaches: (1) C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) — open standard for media provenance metadata using cryptographic signatures; (2) SynthID (Google DeepMind) — invisible watermarking embedded in the generation process. C2PA metadata can be stripped; SynthID watermarks are more robust to transformation but are proprietary. The EU AI Act (Article 50) requires AI-generated content to be machine-detectably marked. Level 3 because watermarking tooling is still maturing and robustness to adversarial removal is an active research area. |

## Related Standards & References

- [C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity)](https://c2pa.org/) — open standard for media provenance and authenticity
- [SynthID (Google DeepMind)](https://deepmind.google/technologies/synthid/) — invisible watermarking for AI-generated content
- [EU AI Act, Article 50 — Transparency obligations for providers and deployers of certain AI systems](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj) — requires machine-readable marking of AI-generated content
- [Content Authenticity Initiative (CAI)](https://contentauthenticity.org/) — industry initiative for content provenance based on C2PA
- [NIST AI 100-4 (Synthetic Content)](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence) — NIST work on reducing risks of synthetic content
- [National CSAM reporting requirements](https://www.missingkids.org/gethelpnow/cybertipline) — legal obligations for detecting and reporting CSAM in generated content

## Open Research Questions

- How robust are current watermarking schemes to adversarial removal? Can watermarks survive screenshot-and-reupload workflows?
- How should consent verification work at scale for voice cloning and face generation? Is a consent database technically feasible?
- What similarity threshold constitutes copyright infringement in generated visual and audio content?
- How should generative media safeguards evolve as video generation quality approaches photorealism?
- Should AI-generated media provenance be mandatory for all generative systems, or only for systems above a certain capability threshold?
