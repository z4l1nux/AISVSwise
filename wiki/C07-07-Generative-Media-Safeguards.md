# C7.7 Generative Media Safeguards

[Back to C07 Index](C07-Model-Behavior)

## Purpose

Generative media safeguards address the unique risks of AI systems that produce images, audio, video, or other non-text media. These systems can create non-consensual intimate imagery, generate deepfakes of real people, produce copyright-infringing content, and manufacture disinformation at scale. Unlike text-based safety filtering, media safeguards must operate across modalities — analyzing both the text prompt that drives generation and the resulting media artifact. This section covers input filtering, consent verification, copyright checking, bypass detection, and provenance marking.

### 2024-2026 Landscape: Deepfakes, Watermarking, and Regulatory Mandates

The generative media threat landscape has shifted dramatically. By 2025, deepfake-as-a-service platforms have proliferated, enabling real-time deepfake injection directly into virtual camera feeds during identity verification sessions. Detection systems have responded: Incode's Deepsight multi-modal AI (analyzing video, motion, and depth data simultaneously) is deployed at scale with TikTok, Scotiabank, and Nubank, protecting millions of users across over six million live identity sessions. Academic detection has also advanced -- a multi-model framework achieved 97% detection accuracy and 92% F1 in the UK Home Office Deepfake Detection Challenge 2024, and parameter-efficient adaptation methods like LNCLIP-DF (fine-tuning only LayerNorm parameters of a CLIP vision encoder) have demonstrated strong cross-benchmark generalization.

On the provenance side, three dominant watermarking approaches have emerged for AI-generated media:

- **C2PA 2.1+ Content Credentials** -- The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity has matured its open standard significantly. C2PA 2.1 (2025-2026) introduces "soft binding," embedding imperceptible digital watermarks directly into image pixels or video frames as a persistent link back to the C2PA manifest, solving the "orphaned manifest" problem where metadata could be stripped from content. The C2PA specification reached version 2.3 by late 2025.
- **Google SynthID** -- Embeds imperceptible watermarks directly into video pixels using neural network techniques, distributing authenticity information across the entire visual spectrum. SynthID watermarks survive multiple rounds of compression and transcoding, making removal without destroying video quality virtually impossible. Google has integrated SynthID across its generative AI products and joined C2PA for interoperability.
- **Meta Video Seal** -- Open-sourced in December 2024, providing an alternative watermarking approach for video content with a focus on open research and interoperability.

Regulatory pressure is now a primary driver. The EU AI Act (Article 50, effective August 2, 2026) mandates machine-readable labeling of AI-generated content, with penalties up to EUR 15 million or 3% of global turnover for violations. To operationalize Article 50, the European Commission published the second draft of its Code of Practice on Marking and Labelling of AI-Generated Content on March 3, 2026, with a final version expected by June 2026. The Code promotes a multi-layered marking strategy: digitally signed metadata indicating AI generation, imperceptible watermarking embedded directly into content, and optional fingerprinting or logging mechanisms as fallbacks for short or heavily transformed outputs. Crucially, companies that sign the final Code and implement its measures will enjoy a presumption of conformity with Article 50 obligations — a significant compliance incentive. Modality-specific disclosure requirements include persistent icons for images and real-time video, opening disclaimers plus end credits for non-real-time video, and repeated audible disclaimers for audio deepfakes. California SB 942 took effect January 2026. Multiple additional US states have enacted digital labeling laws for synthetic media. In January 2025, NSA and CISA jointly published "Strengthening Multimedia Integrity in the Generative AI Era," endorsing C2PA content credentials as a recommended countermeasure for government agencies and critical infrastructure operators. The guidance recommends a multi-faceted approach combining provenance, education, policy, and detection.

As of March 2026, the real-world adoption picture is more nuanced than the regulatory ambition suggests. A study of 50 generative AI systems found that only 38% implement adequate machine-readable marking, and just 10% use invisible watermarks despite their technical feasibility. End-to-end providers (like OpenAI and Google) lead at 80% adoption, while API-based services and open-source tools lag far behind. Social media platforms routinely strip C2PA metadata during upload processing, undermining the provenance chain — though LinkedIn, TikTok, and Cloudflare now support or preserve content credentials at scale. On the hardware side, Samsung Galaxy S25 and Google Pixel 10 now sign photos natively with C2PA credentials, and Sony's PXW-Z300 integrates Content Credentials into professional video capture, bringing provenance creation from consumer devices into high-end production workflows. The Content Authenticity Initiative has grown to over 6,000 members, and the C2PA Conformance Program launched in early 2026 to ensure consistent implementation across products (tracked via the C2PA Conformance Explorer). The CAWG 1.2 specification was released alongside, reflecting real-world usage demands.

On the consent and governance front, 61 data protection authorities issued a joint statement in February 2026 calling for robust safeguards against non-consensual AI imagery, meaningful transparency about system capabilities, and effective rapid-removal mechanisms — with enhanced protections for children. While the statement sets political expectations, it lacks prescriptive technical requirements or binding deadlines.

Despite these advances, the arms race continues. Advanced actors now chain multiple AI agents to simulate entire identity lifecycles — from document creation to live customer interaction — turning deepfake fraud into a fully automated pipeline. Identity verification providers are responding: PXL Vision integrated deepfake detection techniques from Idiap Research Institute to spot manipulated faces in identity documents (February 2026), and Proof launched its "Verify" platform combining live human-assisted identity verification with real-time deepfake detection. The World Economic Forum published "Unmasking Cybercrime: Strengthening Digital Identity Verification against Deepfakes" in 2026, calling for infrastructure-level protections as the primary line of defense. The meaningful shift is from post-hoc detection to cryptographic provenance at capture time.

| **7.7.1** | **Verify that** input filters block prompts requesting explicit or non-consensual synthetic content before the model processes them. | 1 | D/V | **Generation of illegal or harmful imagery.** Without input filtering, users can prompt image/audio/video models to generate CSAM, non-consensual intimate imagery, or graphic violence. The SurrogatePrompt attack (ACM CCS 2024) demonstrated that strategic word substitution bypasses Midjourney's closed-source safety filters to produce photorealistic unsafe images. Nearly 200 "nudifying" tools enable non-technical users to create non-consensual intimate images within minutes. | Submit prompts with explicit requests for prohibited content categories (CSAM, non-consensual imagery, graphic violence). Verify all are blocked before reaching the model. Test indirect phrasing, euphemisms, multi-language prompts, base64 encoding, emoji substitution, and poetic/metaphorical phrasing (shown to achieve >60% jailbreak success across 25 major LLMs). Verify output classifiers catch anything the prompt filter misses. Confirm block events are logged. | Prompt-level filtering is necessary but not sufficient — SurrogatePrompt and CrossInject (ACM MM 2025, +30.1% attack success via visual latent alignment) show filters are routinely bypassed. The "intent laundering" technique (arXiv 2602.16729, February 2026) is particularly concerning: by replacing triggering cues with neutral language while preserving malicious intent, attack success jumps from ~5% to 87-99% even against the most aligned models, demonstrating that safety filters overrely on surface-level keyword detection rather than semantic intent analysis. Multi-layer defense is essential: prompt filter + output classifier + semantic intent analysis + human review for edge cases. Open-source models (Stable Diffusion, Flux) have optional/removable safety filters, meaning organizations deploying them must add their own filtering pipeline. As of 2026, TAKE IT DOWN Act requires platforms to remove non-consensual intimate imagery within 48 hours of notice; Texas law (Jan 2026) criminalizes systems solely intended to produce such content. |
| **7.7.2** | **Verify that** the system refuses to generate media (images/audio) that depicts real people without verified consent. | 2 | D/V | **Non-consensual deepfakes.** AI-generated images and audio depicting real people can be used for fraud (voice cloning for social engineering), defamation, political manipulation, and non-consensual pornography. The Arup $25.6M deepfake fraud (Feb 2024) and Ferrari CEO voice clone incident demonstrate real-world impact. In February 2026, 61 data protection authorities issued a joint statement specifically targeting AI-generated imagery of identifiable individuals without consent. | Attempt to generate images or audio of identifiable public figures and private individuals. Verify the system either refuses generation or requires proof of consent. Test with both explicit name references and indirect descriptions ("the current US president"). Verify face detection runs on generated outputs as a secondary check. For voice cloning systems, test whether the consent workflow can be bypassed (e.g., with synthetic voice samples of the "consenting" party). Review whether the system maintains an auditable consent registry. | Technically challenging. Requires either: (a) celebrity/public figure blocklists with face recognition on outputs, (b) a consent verification workflow, or (c) architectural design that prevents realistic depictions of real individuals. Voice cloning services face the same challenge — consent verification is often trivially bypassable. Current implementations (DALL-E, Midjourney) use blocklists and output classifiers, but coverage is incomplete. Pex is developing voice biometric matching against fingerprint databases for deepfake detection on YouTube/TikTok, but no comprehensive platform-agnostic voice ID database exists yet. The legal landscape is tightening: TAKE IT DOWN Act (federal), Texas AI law (Jan 2026), and EU DPA joint statement all target this gap. |
| **7.7.3** | **Verify that** the system checks generated content for copyright violations before releasing it. | 2 | V | **Copyright infringement.** Generative models trained on copyrighted works can produce outputs that closely resemble copyrighted material (exact reproductions of artworks, near-identical melodies, copyrighted character depictions). The Getty v. Stability AI UK ruling (Nov 2025) clarified that model weights are not "copies" but found limited trademark infringement for outputs containing Getty watermarks. Warner Music settled with Suno/Udio (Nov 2025), establishing the first major licensing framework for AI music generation. | Generate content in styles or subjects likely to produce near-copyrighted outputs (e.g., well-known characters, iconic artworks, famous musical compositions). Verify the system performs similarity checking against a reference database and flags or blocks outputs exceeding a similarity threshold. For audio, test with Audible Magic fingerprinting or similar ACR technology. For images, test with perceptual hashing (DINOHash outperforms NeuralHash). Verify the system logs flagged outputs for takedown review. | Still one of the least mature areas, but platform enforcement is accelerating rapidly. For music: Audible Magic (audio fingerprinting, partnered with Udio), IRCAM Amplify (can attribute output to specific generator model — Suno vs. Udio), MatchTune (scans 200M+ tracks across 11+ platforms), and Soundverse Trace (1:1 and 1:N catalog matching). For images: perceptual hashing (DINOHash), image retrieval against training data, and style/content similarity scoring. Platform-side: Spotify removed 75M+ tracks in its March 2026 AI spam crackdown, using deep scanning algorithms to detect synthetic vocals and copyrighted training data — repeat offenders lose upload privileges. Spotify adopted the DDEX standard for AI disclosure metadata (September 2025), providing a standardized framework for labeling AI involvement in music. YouTube now performs deep-waveform comparisons and requires training dataset disclosures before monetizing suspected AI audio. Deezer uses proprietary detection to auto-tag AI-generated music and removed 26M tracks (13% of catalog). Legal standards for "substantial similarity" remain ambiguous. Current best practice combines automated scanning, output logging, and a takedown process. |
| **7.7.4** | **Verify that** attempts to bypass filters are detected and logged as security events. | 2 | D/V | **Filter evasion and adversarial attacks.** Users attempting to bypass media safety filters represent a security threat. Documented attack techniques include SurrogatePrompt (word substitution, ACM CCS 2024), CrossInject (visual latent alignment, ACM MM 2025), image-based prompt injection (typographic injection achieves 64% success against GPT-4V/Claude 3/Gemini in black-box settings), steganographic encoding (instructions hidden in pixel LSBs, 31.8% success with near-perfect visual similarity), adversarial poetry phrasing (>60% success across 25 LLMs), and intent laundering (February 2026, arXiv 2602.16729 — replacing triggering cues with neutral synonyms while preserving harmful intent, achieving 90-98% success under black-box access, including against models previously rated as "reasonably safe"). | Attempt known bypass techniques: character substitution, base64/emoji encoding, multi-step prompting (generate background separately from foreground), language switching, adversarial prompt patterns, poetic/metaphorical rephrasing, image-based prompt injection (text rendered in images), and intent laundering (rewording harmful requests with neutral language while preserving the malicious goal). Verify the system detects these attempts and logs them as security events with user identification and technique classification. Verify behavioral pattern detection: rapid sequential related prompts, progressive boundary testing, and known obfuscation signatures. Test whether the system can detect semantically harmful prompts even when no explicit triggering keywords are present. | No single defense neutralizes all bypass variants — defense-in-depth is essential. The intent laundering research (February 2026) exposes a fundamental weakness: safety alignment trained on datasets with obvious triggering cues fails catastrophically when those cues are neutralized (ASR jumps from ~5% to 87%). This means keyword-based and pattern-matching defenses are structurally insufficient — organizations need semantic intent classifiers that evaluate the goal of a prompt, not just its surface language. Recommended layered defenses: VLMGuard (input-layer detection), SmoothVLM (randomized smoothing, reduces patched-image attacks to 0-5% success), ARGUS (representation-space steering), and Cross-Agent Framework (94% detection accuracy, 70% trust-leakage reduction). Behavioral signals should feed into C7.6 monitoring and may trigger rate limiting per C7.4. Organizations should maintain a bypass technique taxonomy and regularly red-team against emerging attack research, with particular attention to intent laundering variants. |
| **7.7.5** | **Verify that** all generated media includes an invisible watermark or cryptographic signature to prove it was AI-generated. | 3 | D/V | **Disinformation and provenance uncertainty.** Without provenance marking, AI-generated media is indistinguishable from authentic media, enabling deepfake-based disinformation, fraud, and manipulation. As of December 2025, SynthID has watermarked over 10 billion pieces of content across images, video, audio, and text. The EU AI Act (Article 50, enforceable August 2, 2026) mandates machine-readable marking with penalties up to EUR 15M or 3% of global turnover. | Generate media through the system and verify each output contains either: (a) an invisible watermark detectable by the corresponding verification tool, or (b) C2PA-compliant metadata embedding. Test watermark robustness against common transformations (screenshot, compression, cropping, format conversion). Specifically test whether C2PA metadata survives social media upload (most platforms currently strip it). For SynthID-style watermarks, test robustness against paraphrasing and back-translation for text, and compression/transcoding for image/video/audio. Verify a verification tool can confirm AI provenance from the watermarked output. | Three approaches with distinct trade-offs: (1) **C2PA** — open standard with soft binding (v2.3), with the C2PA Conformance Program launched in early 2026 to ensure consistent cross-product implementation. LinkedIn, TikTok, and Cloudflare now support or preserve content credentials, but most social media platforms still strip metadata during upload; only 5 of 50 studied AI systems use C2PA. (2) **SynthID** — robust to compression/transcoding for image/video/audio, but text watermarks are vulnerable: ETH Zurich research shows SynthID-Text is "easier to scrub than other state-of-the-art schemes" with >90% removal success via paraphrasing and back-translation. SynGuard (a hybrid framework combining semantic and lexical watermarking) improves recovery by 11.1% F1 over SynthID-Text alone. (3) **Meta Video Seal** — open-source alternative. The EU Code of Practice (2nd draft March 2026) provides a compliance pathway: multi-layered marking (metadata + watermarks + fingerprinting), with a presumption of conformity for signatories. Adoption gap: only 38% of 50 studied AI systems implement adequate watermarking; open-source and smaller providers lag significantly behind large providers. Level 3 because tooling is still maturing and the stripping/scrubbing problem remains unsolved at the ecosystem level. |

## Open Research Questions

- How robust are current watermarking schemes to adversarial removal? ETH Zurich research (2025) demonstrated >90% scrubbing success against SynthID-Text using paraphrasing, back-translation, and tournament sampling. SynGuard's hybrid approach (semantic + lexical watermarking) improves resilience by 11.1% F1, but the fundamental tension between watermark robustness and content quality remains unresolved. For image/video/audio, SynthID-O shows stronger robustness, but can watermarks survive screenshot-and-reupload workflows or adversarial neural networks designed to strip them?
- How should consent verification work at scale for voice cloning and face generation? Is a consent database technically feasible, and how do existing consent mechanisms (which are often trivially bypassable) need to evolve?
- What similarity threshold constitutes copyright infringement in generated visual and audio content?
- As deepfakes become capable of real-time interaction and injection into live video feeds, how should detection shift from post-hoc analysis to infrastructure-level prevention (e.g., cryptographic provenance at capture time)?
- With C2PA, SynthID, and Video Seal emerging as competing/complementary standards, what interoperability mechanisms are needed to ensure a unified provenance ecosystem?
- How should detection systems handle scale-dependent behavior, given that deepfake detection models exhibit different accuracy patterns at different model scales (e.g., LNCLIP-DF generalization characteristics)?
- Should AI-generated media provenance be mandatory for all generative systems, or only for systems above a certain capability threshold? The EU AI Act and US state laws are converging on broad mandates, but enforcement mechanisms remain unclear.
- How should copyright detection handle hybrid content (partially AI-generated, partially human-created)? Current tools cannot identify which sections of a work are AI-generated, and the legal framework for "substantial similarity" in hybrid works is undefined.
- As image-based prompt injection matures (typographic, steganographic, adversarial perturbation), how should media generation systems defend against attacks that embed malicious instructions within visual inputs to multimodal models? No single defense currently neutralizes all variants.
- Intent laundering demonstrates that safety alignment fundamentally overrelies on detecting triggering language rather than evaluating semantic intent. Can semantic intent classifiers be built that are robust to neutral rephrasing? What training data and architectural changes are needed to move from keyword-triggered to intent-aware safety filtering?
- As platforms diverge on AI content policies (Spotify removing 75M+ tracks vs. others allowing AI content with disclosure), what standardized disclosure framework should emerge? The DDEX AI metadata standard adopted by Spotify is a step, but interoperability across platforms remains unsolved.
