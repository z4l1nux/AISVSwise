# C6.1 Pretrained Model Vetting & Origin Integrity

> **Parent:** [C06: Supply Chain Security](C06-Supply-Chain.md) | **Requirements:** 5 | **IDs:** 6.1.1–6.1.5

## Purpose

Pretrained models downloaded from public hubs (Hugging Face, Model Zoo, Civitai, etc.) represent one of the highest-risk supply-chain entry points in AI systems. Unlike traditional software dependencies, model weight files can encode arbitrary executable code (via pickle deserialization), contain hidden backdoor triggers, or carry undisclosed licensing or data-provenance obligations. This section ensures every third-party model is authenticated, scanned, and reviewed before it enters the organization's pipeline.

## Requirements

| # | Requirement | Level | Role | Threat Mitigated | Verification Approach | Gaps / Notes |
|---|-------------|:-----:|:----:|-----------------|----------------------|--------------|
| **6.1.1** | **Verify that** every third-party model artifact includes a signed origin-and-integrity record identifying its source, version, and integrity checksum. | 1 | D/V | **Tampered or substituted model weights.** An attacker replaces a legitimate model file on a public hub with a trojaned version. Without checksums, the swap is invisible (MITRE ATLAS AML.T0010.002). The OWASP LLM Top 10 2025 elevated Supply Chain from #5 to **LLM03**, reflecting the growing severity of model-origin attacks. **Model namespace reuse** is a newer vector: Palo Alto Unit 42 showed (Feb 2025) that when a model author deletes their Hugging Face account, anyone can re-register the namespace and upload a malicious replacement. Pipelines referencing the old name silently pull the attacker's model — researchers demonstrated RCE on Google Vertex AI and Azure AI Foundry this way. Google now runs daily scans for orphaned model references. The EU AI Act GPAI obligations (in force August 2025) require providers to maintain technical documentation and share provenance information with downstream users. | Check that every imported model has a corresponding manifest containing: publisher identity, version tag, and SHA-256/SHA-512 hash. Verify the signature chain back to the publisher's public key. As of June 2025, the **OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS) specification** provides a formal, PKI-agnostic standard for model signing — it supports bare keys, PKI certificate chains, and Sigstore keyless signing, and uses a detached signature format covering weights, configs, and tokenizers in a single verifiable unit. NVIDIA has been signing all NGC catalog models with OMS since March 2025; Google is prototyping OMS integration for Kaggle. Pin models to specific commit hashes (not just names) to defend against namespace-reuse attacks. Cross-reference against CycloneDX ML-BOM or SPDX AI profile for standardized provenance metadata. | The OMS specification is a significant step forward, but adoption beyond NVIDIA and Google remains limited as of early 2026. Most Hugging Face publishers still do not sign. Organizations should self-sign upon first import and treat the internal hash as the trust anchor. Always pin model references to commit hashes, not just namespace/name — name-based trust is demonstrably broken. NIST AI RMF "Map" function explicitly calls for AI-BOMs, acknowledging traditional SBOMs do not capture model-specific risks. EU AI Act enforcement powers take effect August 2026, with fines for non-compliance. |
| **6.1.2** | **Verify that** models are scanned for malicious layers or Trojan triggers using automated tools before import. | 1 | D/V | **Pickle deserialization RCE and embedded malware.** PyTorch `.pt`/`.bin` files use Python pickle, which can execute arbitrary code on load. Backdoored models may also contain hidden trigger patterns that activate on specific inputs. (CVE-2024-3568 in Hugging Face Transformers, JFrog 2024 Hugging Face malware findings). **2025 incidents:** (1) *nullifAI attack* (Feb 2025) — ReversingLabs discovered malicious models on Hugging Face using broken/corrupt pickle streams and 7z compression to evade Picklescan; payloads were reverse shells connecting to hardcoded IPs. Hugging Face removed them within 24 hours. (2) *MalHug detection pipeline* found 91 malicious models and 9 malicious dataset-loading scripts across 705K+ models on Hugging Face. (3) JFrog disclosed three critical PickleScan zero-days (CVE-2025-10155, CVE-2025-10156, CVE-2025-10157, all CVSS 9.3) exploiting divergence between how PickleScan parses and PyTorch loads files — patched in PickleScan 0.0.31 (Sep 2025). | Run **multiple** scanning tools — no single scanner is sufficient given demonstrated bypass rates. Primary tools: **Protect AI ModelScan** (supports H5, Pickle, SavedModel; commercial Guardian extends to 35+ formats), **Promptfoo ModelAudit** (42+ formats, allowlist-first architecture, SARIF output for CI/CD, detected 16 issues vs. ModelScan's 3 in head-to-head testing), **Trail of Bits Fickling** (pickle decompilation + allowlist scanner). Prefer SafeTensors/ONNX format over pickle-based formats. For Trojan detection, run Neural Cleanse, ABS, or MNTD scans on candidate models. | Trojan-trigger scanning (Neural Cleanse, etc.) is computationally expensive and has known false-negative rates. **Pickle scanning alone is insufficient:** the nullifAI attack and JFrog zero-days proved that blocklist-based scanners can be evaded. Defense-in-depth requires: (1) multiple scanners with different detection architectures, (2) format restrictions (SafeTensors-only policy where feasible), and (3) sandboxed model loading. As of March 2025, only ~42% of Hugging Face models use SafeTensors — an empirical study (arxiv 2501.02170) found that many maintainers ignore automated SafeTensors conversion PRs, so the ecosystem transition is slower than hoped. HiddenLayer's 2026 AI Threat Landscape Report found that malware in public model and code repositories is now the most-cited source of AI-related breaches (35% of reported incidents), and one in eight AI breaches involves agentic systems pulling models autonomously. The academic paper "The Art of Hide and Seek" (2025) demonstrates further stealthy pickle poisoning techniques. |
| **6.1.3** | **Verify that** model licenses, export-control tags, and data-origin statements are recorded in an AI BOM entry. | 2 | V | **Legal and compliance risk from undisclosed training data or license terms.** Models trained on copyrighted, GDPR-covered, or export-controlled data can expose the organization to lawsuits or regulatory action. The EU AI Act GPAI obligations (effective August 2025) require providers to publish sufficiently detailed summaries of training-data content, comply with EU copyright law, and share documentation with regulators and downstream deployers. Non-compliance fines apply from August 2026. NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile) extends the AI RMF to explicitly address training data transparency, model provenance, and third-party dependency risk for generative AI systems. | Audit the AI BOM for each model artifact. Confirm it includes: SPDX license identifier, data-origin summary (e.g., "trained on Common Crawl + proprietary corpus"), and export-control classification (EAR/ITAR if applicable). Cross-reference against the model card if one exists. Use **CycloneDX ML-BOM** (stable through v1.7, ratified as ECMA-424 2nd Edition, December 2025) or **SPDX 3.0.1** AI profile as the BOM format. CycloneDX v1.7 added a **Citations** element that lets BOM authors declare exactly which tool, build system, or person contributed each piece of provenance data — useful for audit trails showing who attested to training-data composition. Tools like Promptfoo ModelAudit can generate SBOMs and extract license information as part of scanning. | Many open-weight models lack clear data-origin disclosures. Organizations may need to perform independent provenance research. EU AI Act GPAI obligations are now enforceable — providers of models placed on market before August 2025 must comply by August 2027. NIST AI RMF and NIST AI 600-1 explicitly call for AI-BOMs. The systemic-risk tier (models exceeding 10^25 FLOP) carries additional safety and security obligations. CycloneDX v1.7 Citations provide the granular attribution needed for regulatory evidence, but tooling to auto-populate these fields is still emerging. |
| **6.1.4** | **Verify that** high-risk models (e.g., publicly uploaded weights, unverified creators) remain quarantined until human review and sign-off. | 2 | D/V | **Deployment of unvetted or backdoored models.** Automated pipelines may pull and deploy models without human judgment, allowing compromised artifacts to reach production. The MalHug study found 91 malicious models across 705K+ on Hugging Face — demonstrating that public hubs contain real threats at scale, not just theoretical risks. The Rapid7 "From .pth to p0wned" analysis (2025) documented end-to-end exploitation chains from model download to remote access trojan deployment. | Confirm that a quarantine zone (separate registry, staging namespace, or approval gate) exists. Verify that models from unverified publishers cannot progress past the quarantine stage without a documented human approval record (e.g., Jira ticket, signed review form). Run automated scanning (ModelScan, ModelAudit, Fickling) during quarantine. Integrate Palo Alto AI Runtime Security or Black Duck AI model scanning (released Oct 2025) for enterprise-grade quarantine workflows. | Defining "high-risk" criteria requires organizational policy. Suggested triggers: unknown publisher, no model card, pickle format, model size anomalies, flagged by automated scanning, namespace squatting indicators, or **recently re-registered namespaces** (the Unit 42 namespace-reuse attack exploits exactly this gap). Commercial tools like Protect AI Guardian, Black Duck, and HiddenLayer Model Scanner now offer managed quarantine-and-scan pipelines with integration into model registries like Databricks Unity Catalog. Sonatype's 2026 Software Supply Chain Report documented over 454,600 new malicious packages across ecosystems including Hugging Face, bringing the cumulative total of known malware to over 1.23 million packages. |
| **6.1.5** | **Verify that** transfer-learning fine-tunes pass adversarial evaluation to detect hidden behaviors. | 3 | D | **Sleeper-agent attacks and backdoor persistence through fine-tuning.** Research (Anthropic "Sleeper Agents" 2024, BadNets, TrojAI) shows that backdoors in base models can survive fine-tuning. An attacker contributes a poisoned base model; the victim fine-tunes it, unknowingly inheriting the backdoor. The "Models Are Codes" study (2024) systematically measured malicious code poisoning across pre-trained model hubs, confirming that poisoned models are a real and growing phenomenon, not just a lab demonstration. | Run adversarial test suites against fine-tuned models, including: trigger-pattern sweeps (randomized input perturbations), activation-clustering analysis, and behavioral probes for known attack patterns. Compare fine-tuned model behavior against a clean baseline on a held-out adversarial evaluation set. The PickleBall framework (2025) proposes secure deserialization of pickle-based ML models as a complementary control. | This is computationally expensive and currently lacks standardized tooling. The TrojAI program (IARPA/NIST) is developing benchmarks. Most organizations will need to build custom adversarial evaluation pipelines. Level 3 is appropriate given the tooling gap. EU AI Act systemic-risk GPAI models (>10^25 FLOP) face additional safety evaluation requirements that may drive tooling maturity in this area. |

## Implementation Maturity

| Requirement | Maturity | Notes |
|-------------|:---:|-------|
| 6.1.1 Signed origin and integrity records | Emerging → Early Adoption | The **OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS) specification** (June 2025) provides a formal, PKI-agnostic standard with Sigstore keyless signing support. NVIDIA has signed all NGC models since March 2025; Google is prototyping for Kaggle. Contributors include Google, HiddenLayer, Red Hat, Intel, Meta, IBM, and Microsoft. Despite this, most Hugging Face publishers still don't sign — organizations must self-sign upon import and pin to commit hashes. The namespace-reuse attack (Unit 42, Feb 2025) shows name-based trust is broken. |
| 6.1.2 Malicious layer/Trojan scanning | Maturing | Multiple scanners available: ModelScan, ModelAudit (42+ formats, allowlist-first), Fickling (pickle allowlist), HiddenLayer Model Scanner (commercial, Databricks Unity Catalog integration). JFrog PickleScan zero-days (CVE-2025-10155/56/57) and nullifAI proved no single scanner is reliable. SafeTensors adoption is only ~42% on Hugging Face as of March 2025 (empirical study arxiv 2501.02170). HiddenLayer's 2026 report: 35% of AI breaches traced to malicious models in public repos. Defense-in-depth required: multiple scanners + SafeTensors format restriction + sandboxed loading. |
| 6.1.3 License/export-control/data-origin in AI BOM | Maturing | CycloneDX v1.7 (ECMA-424 2nd Edition, December 2025) adds Citations for granular data-provenance attribution. SPDX 3.0.1 AI profiles also maturing. NIST AI 600-1 (Generative AI Profile) explicitly requires training data transparency and provenance. OWASP AIBOM Generator scans repos for HF model usage. Gap: many open-weight models lack clear data-origin disclosures, and CycloneDX Citations auto-population tooling is still emerging. |
| 6.1.4 Quarantine until human review | Maturing | Protect AI Guardian, Black Duck (Oct 2025), and HiddenLayer Model Scanner offer managed quarantine-and-scan pipelines. Palo Alto AI Runtime Security provides enterprise workflows. MalHug found 91 malicious models across 705K+ on HF. Sonatype's 2026 report: over 454,600 new malicious packages across ecosystems, bringing cumulative known malware past 1.23 million. Quarantine must also check for namespace reuse — recently re-registered accounts are a red flag. |
| 6.1.5 Adversarial evaluation of fine-tunes | Emerging | Computationally expensive with no standardized tooling. Neural Cleanse, ABS, MNTD for backdoor detection. NIST TrojAI developing benchmarks. Anthropic "Sleeper Agents" (2024) showed backdoors survive fine-tuning. Level 3 is appropriate given the tooling gap. |

## Open Research Questions

- What is the false-negative rate of current Trojan-detection tools (Neural Cleanse, ABS, MNTD) against state-of-the-art backdoor attacks? The "Art of Hide and Seek" (2025) paper suggests attackers continue to outpace defenses.
- Can model provenance be established for models where training data and training process are not disclosed (closed-weight models accessed via API)?
- How should organizations handle models that are "fine-tune chains" (Model A fine-tuned from Model B fine-tuned from Model C) where provenance may be lost at each step?
- The OpenSSF Model Signing (OMS) specification now exists (June 2025) with NVIDIA and Google as early adopters — but will the broader Hugging Face ecosystem actually adopt it? What incentives or platform enforcement mechanisms are needed to drive signing adoption past the current handful of publishers?
- **How should organizations defend against model namespace reuse attacks?** Pinning to commit hashes helps, but many codebases and documentation still reference models by name alone. Is there a scalable way to detect when a previously trusted namespace has changed ownership?
- **How should organizations layer multiple scanning tools given demonstrated bypasses?** The JFrog PickleScan zero-days (CVE-2025-10155/10156/10157) and the nullifAI evasion show that no single scanner is reliable. What is the right combination of blocklist (PickleScan), allowlist (ModelAudit/Fickling), and format-restriction (SafeTensors-only) controls?
- **Will EU AI Act GPAI obligations drive standardization of model provenance metadata?** The August 2025 requirements for training-data documentation and downstream transparency may create de facto standards, but enforcement details remain unclear until August 2026.
- **Can the PickleBall secure-deserialization approach scale to production workloads?** If so, it could provide a runtime defense complementing static scanning.
